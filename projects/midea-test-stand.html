mechanical design · test engineering

In-house appliance certification test stand

Test Engineering Intern · Midea America

Overview

Midea was sending appliances to external certification agencies every time they needed performance testing done. It was expensive and slow. My project was to build the capability in-house so they wouldn't have to. That meant designing the test apparatus, machining the hardware, assembling a working prototype, and writing the documentation for the team to keep using it after I left. Each test cycle now saves $50K.

What I actually did

I designed the test apparatus in Creo with DFMA in mind from the start. The goal was something a technician could set up repeatedly without needing an engineer in the room, so I kept the part count low and the assembly straightforward.

Once the prototype was together, I ran it through its paces to validate it against design intent before we committed to the documentation. The test protocol I wrote covers setup, instrumentation, measurement sampling, and acceptance criteria. I also did a statistical sampling study to nail down the right sample sizes and measurement frequency for the data to hold up under scrutiny. The SOP was written to be handed off cleanly to the full-time team.

On top of the main project I was leading an intern group doing parallel prototype research and running thermal simulations on some appliance components. Busy internship.

Tools

Creo for CAD and DFMA analysis. Circular saw and SLA 3D printing for airflow components. Thermal simulation software for the parallel work. Standard test instrumentation and data acquisition for validation.

Results

The test stand works. $50K saved per test cycle by keeping certification testing internal. The full documentation package, protocol, sampling study, and SOP, was handed off to the engineering team and is in active use. The intern group deliverables shipped on time too.
